History of the Atom Models & Experiments

Video + exam-focused notes + 35-question practice

Notes (Exam‑ready key points)

Big idea
  • Atomic theory explains that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms, separated by empty space.
  • Across time, models changed as experiments revealed new evidence.
Early concept — Democritus (~500 BC)
  • Proposed that everything is built from indivisible particles (atoms) with empty space between them.
  • No experimental proof—just philosophical reasoning.
Dalton (early 1800s) — Solid sphere model
  • Atoms as solid spheres.
  • Different spheres correspond to different elements.
J. J. Thomson (1897) — Plum pudding model
  • Discovery of electrons showed atoms aren’t solid spheres.
  • Model: a diffuse sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded within.
Rutherford (1909) — Gold foil experiment → Nuclear model
  • Fired positively charged alpha particles at thin gold foil.
  • Most passed straight through, some deflected, a few bounced back → the positive charge is concentrated.
  • Conclusion: a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus with electrons in a surrounding region.
Problem & solution — Why don’t electrons collapse into the nucleus?
  • Rutherford’s model didn’t explain stability—what stops electrons from spiralling inward?
  • Niels Bohr (1913): electrons occupy specific orbits/shells around the nucleus, preventing collapse.
Refinements — Protons and neutrons
  • Further work showed the nucleus contains protons (positive) and later neutrons (neutral; evidenced by James Chadwick).
  • Modern view largely follows the Bohr‑style nuclear model with later refinements.
Core exam tips
  • Know the chronology: Democritus → Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr → (protons & neutrons).
  • Link each model to its evidence (e.g., gold foil → nucleus).
  • Be able to state what each model got right and what later evidence showed was incomplete.
