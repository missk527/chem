Notes (Key points from the speech)

What do we mean by rate of reaction?
  • Rate of reaction = the speed at which reactants are converted into products.
  • Rates vary massively: from very slow (iron rusting, years/decades) to very fast (explosions, fractions of a second), with “typical” reactions in between (e.g., magnesium + acid → gentle H₂ bubbles).
Two equivalent ways to measure rate
  • Measure how fast reactants are used up, or how fast products are formed.
  • Equations:
    • Rate = quantity of reactant used ÷ time taken
    • Rate = quantity of product formed ÷ time taken
  • Quantities can be in grams (g) or cm³; time commonly in seconds (s).
Worked examples (from the speech)
  • Gas volume: 180 cm³ H₂ in 2 minutes → convert 2 min = 120 s → rate = 180/120 = 1.5 cm³·s⁻¹.
  • Mass used: 3 g Mg used up in 4 minutes → 4 min = 240 s → rate = 3/240 = 0.0125 g·s⁻¹.
  • Alternative units: 0.6 mol used in 2 minutes → if answer wanted in mol·min⁻¹: 0.6/2 = 0.3 mol·min⁻¹.
Average vs instantaneous rate
  • Calculated examples above are average rates across the whole reaction.
  • In reality, rate is usually fastest at the start (many reactant particles) and then slows down as reactants are used up.
Interpreting graphs
  • Plot time on the x‑axis, and either mass of reactant remaining or volume of product formed on the y‑axis.
  • Reactant‑mass graph (e.g., 3 g Mg start): begins at 3 g, falls rapidly at first, then less steep as reaction slows.
  • Product‑volume graph: starts at 0, rises steeply at first (lots of product early), then becomes less steep and plateaus when a reactant runs out.
  • Instantaneous rate at a given time = slope (gradient) of the tangent at that point.
Units and exam tips
  • Be careful with units: convert minutes → seconds if the rate is required in per‑second units.
  • Common units: g·s⁻¹, cm³·s⁻¹, mol·s⁻¹, or per minute (e.g., mol·min⁻¹).
  • State units with your final answer.
