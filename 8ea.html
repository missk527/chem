Notes (Key points from the speech)

Why electron arrangement matters
  • Electrons occupy shells around the nucleus (lowest energy shell fills first).
  • Atoms are most stable when their outer (valence) shell is full.
  • Atoms with incomplete outer shells tend to react to gain/lose electrons and achieve a full shell.
Shell capacities (GCSE rule)
  • 1st shell: 2 electrons.
  • 2nd shell: up to 8 electrons.
  • 3rd shell: treat as up to 8 electrons for the first 20 elements.
  • Fill the first shell before the second, etc.
  • Drawing tip: place one electron at top, right, bottom, left before pairing (clockwise) to keep spacing tidy.
Examples from the speech
  • Sodium (Na), atomic number 11 → electrons = 11 → arrangement 2,8,1. Unstable (one in outer shell).
  • Argon (Ar), atomic number 18 → arrangement 2,8,8. Full outer shell → stable (noble gas).
  • Calcium (Ca), atomic number 20 → arrangement 2,8,8,2. To be stable, it can lose two outer electrons → becomes Ca2+ with arrangement 2,8,8 (draw in brackets with charge).
  • Fluorine (F), atomic number 9 → arrangement 2,7. To be stable, it can gain one electron → becomes F− (fluoride) with arrangement 2,8.
Noble gases
  • Group 0 elements already have full outer shells (e.g., Ne, Ar), so they are generally unreactive.
Ions vs atoms (notation)
  • Atoms become positive ions (cations) by losing electrons; negative ions (anions) by gaining electrons.
  • When drawing ions, use square brackets and show the charge at top right (e.g., [Ca]2+, [F]−).
  • Electron-structure shorthand uses commas: e.g., 2,8,1.
