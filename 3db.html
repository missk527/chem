Molecules, Compounds & Mixtures

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Molecules vs. Compounds
  • Molecule: two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds (can be the same element or different elements).
  • Compound: a pure substance made of two or more different elements chemically bonded.
  • Therefore: all compounds are molecules, but not all molecules are compounds (e.g., O2, N2, Cl2 are molecules not compounds).
2) Examples
O2 — oxygen molecule (elemental) N2 — nitrogen molecule (elemental) Cl2 — chlorine molecule (elemental) H2O — water (compound) CO2 — carbon dioxide (compound) He — helium (monatomic, not a molecule)
3) Fixed Ratios & Chemical Formulae
  • In a given compound, elements appear in the same fixed proportions.
  • Use chemical symbols with subscripts for counts.
Water → H₂O (2 H, 1 O) Carbon dioxide → CO₂ (1 C, 2 O) Sulfuric acid → H₂SO₄ (2 H, 1 S, 4 O)

When only one atom of an element is present, no subscript is written (e.g., the “C” in CO₂).

4) Brackets in Formulae

Brackets group atoms just like in maths. A subscript outside the bracket multiplies the entire group.

Calcium hydroxide → Ca(OH)₂ = 1 Ca, 2 × (O + H) → 1 Ca, 2 O, 2 H
5) Giant (Non‑molecular) Compounds & Ratios
  • Some compounds contain millions/billions of atoms in a giant lattice (often held by ionic bonds), so they are not discrete molecules.
  • Formula shows the simplest ratio of ions/atoms, not a single molecule.
Sodium chloride → NaCl (ratio 1:1; e.g., 1×10⁹ Na⁺ with 1×10⁹ Cl⁻ in a crystal)
6) Mixtures
  • A mixture contains two or more substances (elements and/or compounds) that are not chemically combined.
  • No new bonds between substances; they are just physically mixed.
  • Therefore, mixtures can be separated by physical methods such as filtration, crystallization, and distillation.

Example mixture: O2 molecules + NaCl crystals + He atoms + a CO2 molecule in one beaker.
