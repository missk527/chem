Metals and Non-Metals — Periodic Table, Properties & Reactivity

Notes (Rewritten in English)

Location and ions
  • Most elements in the periodic table are metals, mainly found at the bottom left.
  • Metals form positive ions (e.g., Ca²⁺), while non-metals form negative ions or none (e.g., Cl⁻, carbon).
  • Atoms aim for a full outer electron shell to be stable.
  • Elements on the left lose electrons easily; those on the right gain or share them.
Why metals form positive ions
  • Metals have few outer electrons — easy to lose and form positive ions.
  • Example: Calcium (Group 2) → loses 2 outer electrons → Ca²⁺.
  • Non-metals like chlorine would need to lose 7 electrons to be positive — too much energy, so it doesn’t happen.
Reactivity of metals
  • Metals become more reactive further down a group.
  • Lower elements have more electron shells; outer electrons are farther from the nucleus.
  • Weaker attraction between nucleus and electrons → easier to lose electrons → higher reactivity.
  • Example: Lead (Pb) has 6 shells, so it loses outer electrons easily.
Physical properties of metals
  • Metals have metallic bonding — strong bonds responsible for their key properties.
  • They are strong, malleable (can bend or be hammered), and good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Most have high melting and boiling points (many above 2000°C).
  • Other features: shiny and sonorous (ring when struck).
Physical properties of non-metals
  • Dull in appearance, brittle (break easily).
  • Low melting and boiling points; many are gases at room temperature.
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Lower density than metals — lighter for the same volume.
Transition metals
  • Found in the center of the periodic table (green area).
  • Have typical metal properties plus extras:
    • Can form multiple ions (e.g., Cr²⁺, Cr³⁺, Cr⁶⁺).
    • Often form colored compounds (blue, green, orange, etc.).
    • Act as catalysts — speed up reactions without being used up.
  • Examples: Iron (Haber process for ammonia) and Nickel (hydrogenation to make margarine).
