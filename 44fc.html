Hydrogen–Oxygen Fuel Cell (Exam‑ready Notes)

What a fuel cell does

  • Electrochemical device that converts the chemical energy of a fuel + O₂ directly into electrical energy.
  • The most common type uses H₂ (fuel) and O₂ (oxidant) to produce electricity, heat, and H₂O only.

Key structure

  • Electrolyte in the centre (often alkaline KOH in this syllabus) conducts ions.
  • Porous carbon electrodes with a catalyst (↑ surface area, speeds reactions).
  • Anode (−, left) and Cathode (+, right) connected by an external wire for electron flow (anode → cathode).
  • Gas inlets: H₂ enters anode compartment; O₂ enters cathode compartment. Water/heat leave at the cathode outlet.

How it works (sequence)

  • At the anode: H₂ is oxidised → H⁺ ions and e⁻ formed; electrons go through the external circuit.
  • H⁺ (or equivalent ionic species) migrates through the electrolyte to the cathode.
  • At the cathode: O₂ combines with H⁺ and incoming e⁻ to form H₂O.
  • Electron flow through the wire powers the external load (lamp/motor, etc.).

Half‑equations & overall

  • Anode (oxidation): H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻
  • Cathode (reduction): O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O
  • Overall: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (electrical energy released via moving e⁻)

Exam tips

  • In fuel cells the cathode is positive and the anode is negative (opposite of electrolysis sign convention).
  • Fuel is oxidised at the anode; oxidant is reduced at the cathode.
  • A potential difference is set up between electrodes, driving electrons around the circuit.
  • Electrons move through the external circuit; ions move through the electrolyte.

Pros

  • Very clean at point of use: product is water (no CO₂/NOₓ/soot).
  • High efficiency; continuous operation while fuel is supplied.
  • Longer service life than many batteries and less polluting at disposal.

Cons

  • H₂ storage is bulky (low volumetric energy density) and H₂–air mixtures can be explosive.
  • Hydrogen production often needs energy (commonly from fossil fuels), reducing overall environmental benefit.
  • Infrastructure and safe storage/transport add cost and complexity.
Simple distillation (e.g., obtaining pure water from seawater)
  • Apparatus: flask containing the mixture, bung with a thermometer, condenser with a water jacket (cold water in at the bottom, out at the top), receiver (beaker), and a Bunsen burner or other heat source.
  • Process: heat the solution → the target liquid evaporates → vapor is pushed into the condenser → it cools and condenses → pure liquid collects in the beaker.
  • Outcome for seawater: distilled water is collected; salt remains in the flask.
  • Limitation: poor separation if the mixture contains multiple liquids with similar boiling points—more than one will evaporate.
Fractional distillation (for mixtures of liquids)
  • Same basic setup but add a fractionating column between the flask and condenser.
  • The column: packed with glass beads/rods → very high surface area; temperature gradient (hotter at the bottom, cooler at the top).
  • How it works: the lowest-boiling liquid reaches the top first and passes into the condenser. Vapors of higher-boiling liquids condense on the cooler packing and reflux back into the flask.
  • Example: a mixture of methanol (~65 °C), ethanol (~78 °C), and propanol (higher b.p.). Heat to ~65 °C to collect methanol first; then raise to ~78 °C for ethanol; finally collect propanol at the highest temperature.
  • Note: in reality these liquids are colorless; colors in diagrams are just for clarity.
Core exam tips
  • Always feed cold water at the bottom of the condenser to ensure efficient cooling and a filled jacket.
  • Use a thermometer to monitor the boiling point of the vapor being collected.
  • Simple distillation: best for liquid from solution (large b.p. difference or non-volatile solute).
  • Fractional distillation: best for liquid–liquid mixtures with close boiling points.
  • “Reflux” in the fractionating column increases purity of the fraction being collected.
