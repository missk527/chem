Reversible Reactions, Equilibrium & Position of Equilibrium

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

Reversible vs one‑way reactions
  • One‑way reactions are shown with a single arrow (→): reactants turn into products and don’t go back (e.g., CO₂ does not split into C + O₂ in normal conditions).
  • Reversible reactions use a double arrow (⇌): both the forward and backward reactions can occur.
  • Example: NH₄Cl(s) ⇌ NH₃(g) + HCl(g). Top arrow = forward (decomposition), bottom arrow = backward (reformation).

Equilibrium (dynamic)
  • At the start: forward rate is fast (lots of reactant), backward rate is near zero (few products).
  • Over time: forward slows, backward speeds up.
  • Equilibrium is reached when the forward and backward rates are equal.
  • At equilibrium, concentrations stay constant — but not necessarily equal to each other.
  • Molecules still convert both ways; there’s just no overall change (dynamic balance).
Position of equilibrium
  • If there is relatively more products, equilibrium is said to lie to the right.
  • If there is relatively more reactants, equilibrium lies to the left.
  • Changing conditions (e.g., heating/cooling) can shift the position.
  • For NH₄Cl ⇌ NH₃ + HCl: heating drives the decomposition (more products → right), while cooling favors NH₄Cl (left).
Closed system requirement
  • Equilibrium can only be achieved in a closed system — a sealed setup where no reactants or products escape.
  • If gases escape, equal rates cannot be maintained and the system won’t reach equilibrium.
Exothermic vs endothermic directions
  • Every reversible reaction is endothermic in one direction and exothermic in the other.
  • Example: Hydrated copper sulfate ⇌ anhydrous copper sulfate + water.
  • Forward (dehydration) is endothermic: heating blue crystals drives the reaction to the right → white anhydrous powder + water vapor.
  • Backward (hydration) is exothermic: adding water to the powder reforms the blue crystals and releases heat; position shifts left.
