Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

Defining rate of reaction
  • Rate = change in amount of reactant used or product formed per unit time.
  • Units depend on what you measure (e.g., cm³ s⁻¹ for gas volume; g s⁻¹ for mass).
  • Example: 1200 cm³ H₂ in 4 min → mean rate = \(\frac{1200}{4 \times 60}\) = 5 cm³ s⁻¹.
Using a graph of product volume vs time
  • x‑axis = time; y‑axis = volume of gas (e.g., H₂).
  • Curve is steepest at the start (fastest rate), then gradually flattens as reactants are used.
  • When the curve plateaus, reaction has finished (no more product formed).
Mean rate from a graph
  • Pick the time interval, read corresponding y‑values, compute Δy/Δt.
  • Example: first 3 min gives 1200 cm³ → mean rate = \( \frac{1200}{180} \) = 6.67 cm³ s⁻¹.
Instantaneous rate at a particular time
  • Draw a tangent at that point (a line touching the curve with the same gradient).
  • Find gradient = Δy/Δx using two far‑apart points on the tangent.
  • Example at 2 min: Δy ≈ 600 cm³ and Δx ≈ 170 s → rate ≈ 3.53 cm³ s⁻¹.
  • Examiners accept a small range because the tangent is judged by eye.
Reactant‑remaining graph (mass vs time)
  • y‑axis = mass of reactant remaining; starts high and decreases over time.
  • Use the same tangent method for instantaneous rate.
  • Example at 1 min: Δy ≈ 0.72 g; Δx ≈ 100 s → rate ≈ 0.0072 g s⁻¹.
Exam tips
  • Convert minutes → seconds before calculating rates.
  • Make tangents as long as possible to reduce percentage error.
  • Always state units with your rate.
