Notes (Key points from the speech)

Collision theory — the foundation
  • Particles must collide to react, and collisions must have at least the activation energy (Ea).
  • Two drivers of rate at any moment: collision energy and collision frequency.
  • Higher energy and/or more frequent collisions ⇒ more successful collisions ⇒ faster reaction.
Factor 1 — Temperature
  • Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy.
  • They move faster ⇒ collide more often and with more energy.
  • More collisions exceed Ea ⇒ higher rate.
Factor 2 — Concentration (solutions) / Pressure (gases)
  • Both measure how many particles there are per unit volume.
  • Higher concentration/pressure ⇒ particles are closer together ⇒ more frequent collisions ⇒ higher rate.
Factor 3 — Surface area
  • Greater surface area exposes more particles for collision.
  • Example: 3 g magnesium as a powder reacts faster than the same mass as a block (powder has the highest surface area:volume).
Factor 4 — Catalysts
  • Catalyst: substance that speeds up a reaction but is not used up (not listed as reactant/product).
  • On a reaction profile, catalysts lower the activation energy by providing an alternative pathway.
  • More collisions now have ≥ Ea ⇒ more successful collisions.
  • Common catalysts include transition metals (e.g., cobalt, nickel); in biology, enzymes act as catalysts.
Always link back to collision theory
  • When a factor increases rate, explain whether it increases energy of collisions, frequency of collisions, or both.
